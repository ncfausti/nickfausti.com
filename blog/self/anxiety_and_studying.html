Anxiety, ADHD, and Learning

Credit: Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi via https://alifeofproductivity.com/how-to-experience-flow-magical-chart/

Yesterday, after 15 weeks of intense study and focus, I took my final exam for EAS545 - Engineering Entrepreneurship. This course, taught by Dr. Thomas Cassel at Penn Engineering, was one of my favorite courses I've ever taken. Professor Cassel does such an incredible job of encouraging his students to engage with the material, asking us to put ourselves in the shoes of the people we are reading about in the case studies; to really understand and analyze the situations in depth. I loved working through this course and all of the material, and yet, today I am incredibly dissatisfied with my results yesterday. I ended up getting a 79/80 on the material related to the readings and general ideas of the class, and a 0/20 on the section related to venture capital finance/term sheets. I got a 79/100 on the final exam, and this was 15% of my final grade. I will almost certainly not receive a full 'A' that I had worked so hard for all semester. (edit: I ended up getting an ‘A-’.)

Some background, I'm currently pursuing an MSE in Computer and Information Science at Penn. Last Summer, I returned home from travelling to take an accelerated course on Artificial Intelligence as part of my degree. I was extremely interested in the material and enjoyed the class, yet I performed poorly, procrastinating and not dedicating enough time to the assignments/exam. I got a 'C' and was placed on academic probation because that grade brought my GPA below the threshold for being in good standing in the engineering school. This was one of the lowest points for me in recent memory. This experience led me to question (like I have done so many times before) what was going on with me personally. I had left an incredible experience in (Remote Year) early to take this course, and still, I could not bring myself to go through the assignments diligently, instead allowing my anxiety to put things off and rush through them at the last minute.

Along with this, I had been noticing that I would become irrationally irritated by people in coffee shops and public spaces having what others would consider normal level conversations. With noise-canceling headphones (and earbuds inside of those) I would still fixate on the words of the conversations that were happening around me. This would distract me from my work and studying, and it turned out that the only place where I could be somewhat productive was in the deepest corners of the library, with no visual or auditory stimuli. As you can imagine, constantly needing to be in complete silence was not an adequate solution to my problem. There will certainly be times where I would need to be able to focus and work effectively, I would not always be able to be on the 5th floor of Van Pelt library. This, in combination with the generalized anxiety, procrastination, and time-blindness that I have experienced for most of my life, led me to seek professional help. I talked with my PCP and mentioned that all of my four siblings were diagnosed with ADHD when they were younger, and he recommended that I go for neuro-psychological testing to see if I had it as well. Fast-forward a couple of months, my testing resulted in a diagnosis of ADHD-Inattentive subtype, and I started treatment for it.

It was now January--the beginning of the Spring 2020 semester--equipped with a clear plan on how to combat this awful disorder, I’m excited for the possibilities that lie ahead. My results have been remarkable. I no longer procrastinate for assignments, I engage with the readings prior to class and as a result I get so much more out of every lecture. I received a perfect score on my Game Development mid-term and had been getting 9s and 10s on all of my bi-weekly 10-point assignments for Engineering Entrepreneurship. I have been going to office hours and making sure that I’m doing what’s necessary to excel in my courses. There have been a couple of slip-ups throughout the semester where I would submit assignments late or wait until the last minute, but these were the exceptions now, not the norm. They would happen as a direct result of my anxiety getting the better of me. I knew something was going to require more effort than usual, and I would “psyche myself out” and put it on a pedestal of being more difficult, and as a result I would put it off as a way to avoid that all too familiar feeling of discomfort.

This is my mistake. TLDR; This is why I am writing this post. I want to remind myself (and others) to be mindful about the thoughts and emotions we experience while learning difficult subjects. This discomfort can be your guide. Ignore it and it will steer you unconsciously down a path of misery and despair. Embrace it, analyze and understand it, and accept it for what it is, and it can be a powerful tool to inform you of the areas where you need to improve upon your understanding of something. While preparing for my final exam in EAS 545, I had waited until the day before to study. Partly because I was working on another project, partly because I was fairly confident in my understanding of the majority of the material. Key word here being majority. I put off studying for the part that I knew needed more attention because I was letting the discomforting feeling of not understanding neglect the exact material I needed to be paying the most attention to. I won't let this happen again.
